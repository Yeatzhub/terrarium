# 🔮 Oracle Learning: ATR Use Case — Volatility-Adaptive Stops & Sizing

**Topic:** Indicator — Average True Range (ATR)  
**Time:** 15 minutes

---

## What ATR Measures

ATR = Average of True Range over N periods (typically 14)

**True Range** = max of:
- Current High − Current Low
- |Current High − Previous Close|
- |Current Low − Previous Close|

ATR ≠ directional. It measures **volatility** — how much an asset moves, not where it's going.

---

## Core Use Cases

### 1. Volatility-Adjusted Stop Loss (Primary Use)

**Problem:** Fixed $ or % stops get wrecked in high volatility, leave money on table in low volatility.

**Solution:** ATR-based stops that breathe with the market.

| Multiplier | Use Case | Hit Rate |
|------------|----------|----------|
| 1.0× ATR | Tight scalp stops | 40-50% |
| 1.5× ATR | Day trade swings | 30-40% |
| 2.0× ATR | Standard swing stop | 20-30% |
| 3.0× ATR | Position/trend following | 10-20% |

**Formula:**
```
Long Stop  = Entry − (ATR × Multiplier)
Short Stop = Entry + (ATR × Multiplier)
```

**Example:**
- Entry: $100
- 14-period ATR: $2.50
- Using 2× ATR swing stop: $100 − $5 = **$95 stop**

---

### 2. Position Sizing (Secondary Use)

ATR feeds directly into the "R" in position sizing formulas.

**Formula:**
```
Position Size = Account Risk $ / (ATR × Multiplier)

Example:
- Account: $50,000
- Risk 1%: $500
- ATR: $2.50
- Multiplier: 2×
- Risk per share: $5
- Position Size: $500 / $5 = 100 shares
```

**Key Insight:** Higher ATR = smaller position (same dollar risk). Lower ATR = larger position.

---

### 3. Volatility Regime Detection

| ATR Behavior | Regime | Action |
|--------------|--------|--------|
| ATR expanding | Volatility increasing | Widen stops, reduce size |
| ATR contracting | Volatility decreasing | Tighten stops, increase size |
| ATR at 20-day low | Squeeze setup | Prepare for expansion |
| ATR > 2× 20-day avg | High vol spike | Avoid new entries, manage risk |

**ATR Squeeze Setup:**
- ATR at multi-day lows + price compression → explosive move coming
- Set bracket orders outside range, use ATR expansion for target sizing

---

## Practical Implementation

### Step-by-Step Daily Routine

1. **Pre-market:** Check ATR vs. 20-day average ATR
   - ATR > 1.5× avg: Widen all stops 50%, reduce position sizes 30%
   - ATR < 0.7× avg: Tighten stops 20%, increase sizes 15%

2. **Per-trade stop placement:**
   - Calculate 2× ATR from entry
   - Round to nearest support/resistance for cleaner level
   - Never risk more than 1% account on total position

3. **Trailing stops:**
   - Trail at 2-3× ATR behind price (Chandelier Exit)
   - Adjust daily as ATR changes

## ATR vs. Other Volatility Measures

| Measure | Best For | Limitation |
|---------|----------|------------|
| **ATR** | Stop placement, intraday | No directional bias |
| Bollinger Bands | Mean reversion signals | Lagging, assumes normal distribution |
| VIX (options) | Market-wide fear gauge | Not available per-stock |
| Standard Deviation | Statistical models | Outlier sensitive |
| Historical Volatility | Options pricing | Backward-looking |

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using ATR on its own** → It's a volatility tool, not an entry signal. Pair with trend or momentum.

2. **Fixed multiplier across all assets** → Crypto needs 3×+ ATR, blue chips work with 1.5×. Tune per asset class.

3. **Ignoring ATR expansion** → When ATR doubles, your old stop is now a coin flip. Resize immediately.

4. **Calculating with too few periods** → 14-period minimum for stability. 7-period ATR = noise.
